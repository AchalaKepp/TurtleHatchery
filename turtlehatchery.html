Turtle Population:
An Introduction

The turtle population of Sri Lanka is one of the most remarkable and significant in the world. The country's coastal areas act as crucial nesting grounds for several turtle species, including the Loggerhead, Green, Olive Ridley, Hawksbill, and Leatherback turtles. Sri Lanka's dedication to conservation efforts, such as establishing protected areas and implementing sustainable practices, has played an indispensable role in protecting these endangered creatures. The local communities, along with a sizeable contribution from organizations and government initiatives, actively participate in turtle conservation projects, including hatcheries and beach patrols. Through these combined efforts, the turtle population in Sri Lanka has witnessed a gradual recovery, displaying the positive impact of dedicated conservation measures on the survival and preservation of these magnificent sea creatures


Did you know?

Out of the seven species of sea turtles, six are endangered by human activities. The consumption of these turtles and the dumping of wastes and toxins in water bodies and other environments adaptive to the turtles threaten their existence.

Did you know?

Turtles vary significantly in size, ranging up to 2 to 7 feet and weighing from 70 to over 1,500 pounds. The leatherback sea turtle is the largest sea turtle species. It can weigh up to 2,000 pounds. The largest leatherback ever recorded measured 9 feet and weighed 970kg.

Did you know?

There are about 356 species of turtles in the world. There are so many different types of turtles. Popular species include hawksbill turtles, leatherback turtles, flatback turtles, loggerheads, red-eared sliders, western painted turtles, and African side-necked turtles, amongst others.

Turtle Types

Sri Lanka is home to a diverse range of turtle species that inhabit its coastal waters and beaches. The country boasts five main types of turtles:

  1. Loggerhead Turtle
  2. Olive Ridley Turtle
  3. Leatherback Turtle
  4. Green Sea Turtle
  5. Hawksbill Turtle

The Olive Ridley, Loggerhead, Green, Hawksbill, and Leatherback turtles. The Loggerhead turtle, recognized by its large head and powerful jaws, is a less common sight but occasionally nests on Sri Lankan shores. The Olive Ridley turtle, known for its olive-colored shell, is the most common species found in Sri Lanka. The Green turtle, named after its greenish-colored fat, is another popular species that nests along the country's coastlines. The Hawksbill turtle, with its characteristic pointed beak and beautifully patterned shell, is severely endangered but can still be found in Sri Lankan waters. Lastly, the Leatherback turtle, the largest of all sea turtles, occasionally visits Sri Lanka's shores during its migration. These diverse turtle species contribute to the rich marine ecosystem of Sri Lanka and are a demonstration to the country's importance as a nesting and foraging habitat for these magnificent sea creatures

Where can you watch turtles?

Hikkaduwa, Bentota, and Kosgoda are renowned hatcheries in Sri Lanka committed to the conservation and protection of sea turtles. These hatcheries play a significant role in preserving and increasing the turtle population by collecting and incubating turtle eggs, as well as releasing hatchlings back into the ocean. Hikkaduwa Hatchery, located on the southwest coast, is popular for its turtle conservation efforts and provides visitors with educational experiences about these fascinating creatures. Bentota Hatchery, situated on the southern coast, is known for its turtle conservation projects and provides visitors a chance to witness the hatching process and participate in turtle releases. Kosgoda Hatchery, situated in the western coastal region, focuses on conserving and rehabilitating injured turtles, making it a invaluable center for turtle rehabilitation in Sri Lanka. These hatcheries not only contribute to the preservation of sea turtles but also raise awareness among locals and tourists about the importance of protecting these endangered species and their habitats
